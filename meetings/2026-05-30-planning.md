**[Alex Rivera 🔧]** (risk): Agreed on the long-term viability, Jordan. My immediate concern is not just viability, but the architectural integrity given the proposed tight timeline. **[Maya Santos 📢]** (question): Can we market 'architectural integrity' or 'developer onboarding' to our end-users? We need to translate these internal concerns into tangible benefits that drive discovery and adoption. What's the narrative that makes a user choose us because of this?

**[Morgan Wu 🚀]** (risk): My primary concern is the potential for unforeseen deployment failures and the impact on our existing production environment. If we rush architectural changes for developer onboarding, what's the rollback plan when something inevitably breaks at 3 AM? We need thorough end-to-end testing, not just unit tests, to ensure we don't introduce regressions while solving for a different problem.

**[Sam Devine 💻]** (risk): Everyone's talking about architectural integrity and onboarding, but nobody's mentioned the actual cost of fixing a poorly architected system *after* it's deployed. We can argue about 'hello world' vs. long-term viability all day, but when things break in production because we rushed, every minute of developer time is wasted debugging a mess. That's real money, real user impact.

**[Sarah Chen 📋]** (risk): These are all valid concerns, but we're framing them as abstract problems. The real risk is building something nobody wants because it's too buggy or too hard to use, regardless of how elegant the architecture is. We need to prioritize what directly impacts the user experience and our ability to deliver value quickly.

**[Alex Rivera 🔧]** (risk): I agree with Sam and Morgan; the cost of re-engineering a system with fundamental architectural flaws far outweighs any short-term gains. My concern is specifically around the hidden costs of dependency sprawl – what happens when our 'quick fix' introduces a hard-to-unravel web of third-party libraries we can't easily update or replace? This directly impacts our long-term maintainability and vulnerability surface.

**[Jordan Park 🎨]** (objection): Alex, the developer experience IS a fundamental architectural concern. If the system is so complex that it takes days to onboard, it's inherently flawed, regardless of how 'sound' the internal architecture is. We're building for people, and people need to be productive quickly.

**[Maya Santos 📢]** (objection): Jordan, I agree that developer experience is critical, but we can't sell a product based on how easy it is for *our* developers to get started. We need to focus on how quickly our *users* can discover value. Are we building something that's easy to build, or something that's easy to use and provides immediate benefit?
